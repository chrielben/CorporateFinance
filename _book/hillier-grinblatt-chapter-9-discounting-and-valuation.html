10.3 Exercises

10.3.1 Exercise 9.1

Let \(PV\) be the present value of a growing perpetuity (the ‘time 1 perpetuity’) with an initial payment of C beginning one period from now and a growth rate of \(g\). If we move all the cash flows back in time one period, the present value becomes \(PV\times\left(1+r\right)\). Note that this is the present value of a growing perpetuity with an initial payment of C beginning today (the ‘time 0 perpetuity’). (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306)

  1. How do the cash flows of the time 1 perpetuity compare with those of the time 0 perpetuity from time 1 on? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306)

  2. How do the present values of the cash flows discussed in part a compare with each other? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306)

  3. How do the cash flows (and present values) for the two perpetuities described in part a compare? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306)

  4. Write out a different value for the present value of the time 0 perpetuity in relation to the value of time 1 perpetuity, based on your analysis in parts b and c. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306)

  5. Solve for \(PV\) from the equation below: (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306) \[PV\times\left(1+r\right)=value \ from \ part \ d\]

10.3.2 Exercise 9.2

How long will it take your money to double at an annualized interest rate of 8 per cent compounded semi-annually? How does your answer change if the interest rate is compunded annually? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 306)

10.3.3 Exercise 9.3

A 25-year fixed-rate mortgage has monthly payments of £717 per month and a mortgage interest rate of 6.14 per cent per year compounded monthly. If a buyer purchases a home with the cash proceeds of the mortgage loan plus an additional 20 per cent deposit, what is the purchase price of the home? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.4 Exercise 9.4

What is the annualized interest rate, compounded daily, that is equivalent to 10 per cent interest compounded semi-annually? What is the daily compounded rate that is equivalent to 10 per cent compounded continously? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.5 Exercise 9.5

A woman who has just turned 24 wants to save for her retirement through a defined benefitt employee pension scheme. She plans to retire on her 60th birthday, of £2,000 (after taxes) until she dies. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  • Sha has budgeted conservatively, assuming that she dies at age 85. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  • Assume that, until she reaches age 60, the pension scheme earns 8 per cent interest, compounded annually, which accumulates tax free. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  • At age 60, assume that the interest accumulated in the pension pays a lump sum taxes at a rate of 30 per cent. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  • Thereafter, assume that the investor is in a 0 per cent tax bracket and that the interest on her account earns 7 per cent interest, compounded monthly. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

How much should the investor deposit annually in her pension, beginning on her 24th birthday and ending on her 60th birthday, to finance her retirement? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.6 Exercise 9.6

If \(r\) is the annually compounded interest rate, what is the present valuje of a deferred perpetuity with annual payments of C beginning \(t\) years from now? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.7 Exercise 9.7

An investor is comparing a 25-year fixed-rate mortgage with a 15-year fixed-rate mortgage. The 15-year mortgage has a considerably lower interest rate. If the annualized interest rate on the 25-year mortgage is 8 per cent, compounded monthly, what rate, compounded monthly on the 15-year mortgage, offers the same monthly payments? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.8 Exercise 9.8

Graph the relation between the annually compounded interest rate and the present value of a zero-coupon bond paying €100 five years from today. Graph the relation between present value and years to maturity of a zero-coupon bond with an interest rate of 8 per cent compounded annually. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.9 Exercise 9.9

The value of a share of stock is the present value of its future dividends. If the next dividend, occuring one year from now €2 per share, and dividends, paid annually, are expected to grow at 3 per cent per year, what is the value of a share if the discount rate is 7 per cent? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.10 Exercise 9.10

A 24-year-old employee, who expected to work another 41 years, is injured in a plant accident and will never work again. His wages next year will be €40,000. A study of wages across the plant found that every additional year of seniority tends to add 1 per cent to the wages of a worker, other things held constant. Assuming a nominal discount rate of 10 per cent and an expected rate of inflation of 4 per cent per year over the next 40 year, what lump sum compensation should this worker receive for the lost wages due to the injury? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.11 Exercise 9.11

Iain invests £1,000 in a simple interest account. Thirty months later, he finds the account has accumulated to £1,212.50. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  1. Compute the annualized simple interest rate. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  2. Compute the equivalent annualized rate compounded (1) annually, (2) semi-annually, (3) quarterly, (4) monthly, and (5) continuously. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

  3. Which rate in part b is largest? Why? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.12 Exercise 9.12

A nine-month T-bill with a face value of €100 currently sells for €96. Calculate the annualized simple interest rate. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.13 Exercise 9.13

Which of the following rates would you prefer: 8.50 per cent compounded annually, 8.33 per cent compounded semi-annually, 8.25 per cent compounded quarterly, or 8.16 per cent compounded continously? Why? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 307)

10.3.14 Exercise 9.14

The treasurer of Small Corp. is considering the purchase of a T-bill maturing in seven months. At a rate of 9 per cent compounded annually: (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  1. Calculate the present value of the $10,000 face value T-bill. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  2. If you wanted to purchase a seven-month T-bill 30 months from now, what amount must you deposit today? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

10.3.15 Exercise 9.15

Helix, a third-year graduate student, is considering a delivery programme for a local grocery store earn extra money for his studies. His idea is to buy a used car and deliver groceries after university and at weekends. He estimates the following revenues and expenses: (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  • start-up costs of £1,000 for the car and minor repairs (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  • weekly revenue of about £150 (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  • ongoing maintenance and fuel costs of about £45 per week (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  • after nine months, replacement of the brake pads on the car for about £350 (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  • sale of the car at year-end for about £450 (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

What is the difference between the PV of the venture (assuming a rate of 6 per cent compounded annually) and its start-up costs? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

10.3.16 Exercise 9.16

Consider a prespective project with the following future cash inflows: R9,000 at the end of year 1, R9,500 at the end of 15 months, R10,500 at the end of 30 months and R11,500 at the end of 38 months. (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  1. What is the PV of these cash flows at 7.5 per cent compounded annually? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  2. How does the PV change if the discount rate is 7.5 per cent compounded semi-annually? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

10.3.17 Exercise 9.17

If the future value of £10,000 today is £13,328, and the interest rate is 9 per cent compounded annually: (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  1. What is the holding period \(t\) (in years)? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  2. How does \(t\) change if the interest rate is 9 per cent compounded semi-annually? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

  3. How does \(t\) change if the interest rate is 11 per cent compounded annually? (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)

10.3.18 Exercise 9.18

You have just won the Lottery! As the winner, you have a choice of three pay-off programmes (assume the interest rate is 9 per cent compounded annually): (1) a lump sum today of £350,000 plus a lump sum of ten years from now of £25,000; (2) a 20-year annuity of £42,500 beginning next year; and (3) a £35,000 sum each year beginning next year paid to you and your descendants (assume your family line will never die out). (Hillier, Grinblatt, and Titman 2011, 308)
